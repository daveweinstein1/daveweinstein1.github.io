I got started in 1988 when my co-founder and I built the first software company to accelerate Unix graphics with dedicated graphics hardware on PC hardware. We ported the X Window System and accelerated it with gear you wouldn't believe existed 35 years ago. We cut deals with NCR and Unix System Labs, and eventually I sold the technology to Quarterdeck. That was also when I registered x.com, back in 1993, one of only three single-letter .com domains that IANA ever released.

In early 1999, Elon Musk wanted x.com for his new financial services company. He was not well known in those days, but I liked him, so we worked out a deal for cash and equity. Within a year that company merged with Confinity and became PayPal. I moved on to build netdrive.com, one of the first cloud storage services, years before anyone called it "cloud." I negotiated a deal to sell netdrive to Visto and I ended up running business development for them, where I wrote the strategy to pivot the company into mobile email infrastructure. Visto later acquired Good Technology and was itself bought by BlackBerry.

After that I spent several years as a partner in a small investment bank helping tech companies with cross border deals between the US and China, and actually lived in Beijing for a couple of years back around the time of Beijing Olympics in 2008, it was a different world back then. I wrote for New Atlas as a tech journalist, and you can still find some of my old articles on the site if you search for my name. And in the years running up to the pandemic, I started a technology company in Singapore that did deep tech thermodynamics research in partnership with NTU, creating fundamental technology for ultra-efficient air conditioning and atmospheric water generation.

These days I am focused on giving people control over their own computing. The big platforms are useful, but the tradeoff is that your data becomes their product. I think there is room for alternatives.

I am working on two projects. Scaean Gateway is a software network gateway that brings enterprise-class security to regular home and small office networks. You install it once and every device on your network gets protection automatically, no configuration needed. StrixForge turns a Strix Halo PC or laptop into a proper AI workstation without needing a PhD in system administration. Both are about the same idea: serious tools, built for normal people, that do not require handing your data to someone else.

I am a lifelong technophile and I have been building this stuff for almost 40 years. I just think you should own what is yours.
